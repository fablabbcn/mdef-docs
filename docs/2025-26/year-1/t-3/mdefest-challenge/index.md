## Syllabus

The MDEFest Fabrication Challenge is an intensive two-sprint workshop (4 weeks total) designed to prototype and iterate multimodal systemic artifacts through autonomous, self-directed learning. Students work as distributed collectives—collaborating across individual agency—to design hands-on interventions that engage audiences at MDEFest with tangible experiences that integrate analog, biological, and digital modalities.

This challenge prioritizes **autonomy** as a core methodology, where student agency drives research, prototyping, and iteration. Rather than constant instructional support, expert facilitators provide targeted feedback and **technical support two days per week**, creating space for deep making work and collective problem-solving in between.

Multimodal Systemic Artifacts are tangible creations that function as active nodes within a dynamic ecosystem, integrating multiple channels of perception and response—including analog, biological, and digital modalities.
These artifacts embody closed-loop loops of sensing, interpretation, and actuation, fostering emergent behaviors, collective learning, and sustainable innovation across interconnected systems.

**These are intensive weeks to design and produce Prototype / Artifacts that will directly feed into MDEFest by providing engaging, hands‑on demonstrations and interactive experiences showcasing the research projects you arew working**

## Methodology: Two-Sprint Cycle
Structure: Two consecutive two-week sprints, each culminating in a working prototype or iteration of an artifact.

- Sprint 1 (Week 1–2): Research, concept development, and first artifact iteration
- Sprint 2 (Week 3–4): Refinement, scaling, and second artifact iteration or evolved version
- Presentations: Formal presentations on Day 4 (end of Sprint 1) and Day 8 (end of Sprint 2)

Each sprint follows a cycle of make → reflect → iterate → present, allowing teams to test assumptions, incorporate feedback, and develop resilience through failure and adaptation.

## Key Pedagogical Principles

**Autonomy as Foundation**
Students lead their own research and making processes. Facilitators do not prescribe solutions but rather offer critical questions, resources, and technical guidance. This builds ownership, decision-making capacity, and deeper learning.

**Collective & Individualized**

Teams work as distributed collectives—shared projects with individual contributions tracked and valued. Each person brings different skills and perspectives; the challenge is to leverage diversity while maintaining cohesion. Documentation captures both collective process and individual voice.

**Expert Rotation & Puntual Support**

Each week features a rotating panel of guest facilitators (digital fabrication, systems thinking, interaction design, critical theory, etc.) who provide two days of direct support. Their input is strategic, not continuous—students must develop independent agency between visits.

**Making as Inquiry**

Prototyping is not decoration; it is research. Artifacts are tools for testing hypotheses about how people perceive, interact, and learn. Iteration reveals what works and what requires rethinking.

## Deliverables / Outputs
At the end of the Microchallenge, students must submit two key deliverables to document their learning and project development:

**Individual Post**

Personal learning journey. This will serve as a self-assessment and a way to share insights with the broader community.

**Project Repository**

Each group must create a new repository to document the entire development process of their intervention. This will include research, prototypes, iterations, and final outcomes. **The repository should be hosted on Hackster.io**

### Submission Deadline:
All documentation must be submitted by Monday after the challenge sprints to ensure proper reflection and sharing of the process.
